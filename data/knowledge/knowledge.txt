Ransomware attacks often involve encrypting files and demanding payment. Organizations reduce risk by using backups, segmentation, and patching.
Phishing commonly uses fake login pages and urgent messages to steal credentials. MFA and user training reduce successful phishing.
SOC analysts investigate suspicious login attempts by looking for failed logins, unusual locations, and impossible travel patterns.
DNS tunneling can be used for data exfiltration. Detection involves looking for unusual DNS query length, high volume, and strange domains.
SOC triage basics: confirm alert severity, validate it is not a false positive, identify impacted host/user, gather key IOCs, and escalate if business impact or spread risk is high.
Account compromise triage: check recent logins, impossible travel, MFA resets, mailbox forwarding rules, OAuth consent grants, and unusual privilege changes.
Malware alert response: isolate the endpoint, capture process tree, hash, network connections, and persistence methods. Block IOCs, run EDR scan, confirm no lateral movement.
Brute force or password spraying: many failed logins across multiple users from one IP. Mitigate using rate limits, account lockouts, geo-blocking, and MFA enforcement.
Data exfiltration indicators: unusual outbound traffic volume, large uploads to cloud storage, repeated DNS queries with long subdomains, connections to rare domains.
Beaconing detection: periodic outbound connections at regular intervals, small data packets, consistent destination domain/IP, unusual user-agent strings.
Privilege escalation signs: abnormal admin group membership changes, use of service accounts interactively, access to admin shares, new scheduled tasks created.
MITRE mapping: phishing is Initial Access T1566, DNS tunneling relates to Exfiltration over Alternative Protocol T1048, lateral movement involves remote services T1021.
